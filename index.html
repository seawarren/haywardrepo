Structural Inequality

Why a rupture of the Hayward Fault will be catastrophic for Oakland's most vulnerable populations.

The East Bay's Hayward Fault is widely considered the most deadly in the nation.

It courses through the homes and work places of one of the most densely populated places in the nation.

The fault hasn't erupted in more than 150 years, and like most dangeorus fault lines in California, is long overdue for a rupture.

For Oakland's low-income and minority populations, who can't afford supplemental earthquake insurance — estimated by the California Department of Insurance to average upwards of $700 per month per household — such a rupture would spell disaster.

In 2008, Oakland conducted a survey of all the city's residential buildings to identify those that would be considered "soft story" — a notoriously dangerous construction style popular in some of the building's older neighborhoods.

In December, 10 years after that initial survey, city officials finally decided to do something about it.

The survey found that more than 24,000 units in some 1,500 multiunit residential buildings appeared to exhibit signs of soft story engineering — often buildings with commercial retail or parking on the ground floor. These openings leave the upper floors vulnerable to collapse in case of a powerful earthquake, like the kind the Hayward Fault is long overdue to have.
